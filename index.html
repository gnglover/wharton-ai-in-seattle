Core frameworks

BMC

Business model canvas

Map the model first. Audit each block for decision inefficiencies. Then ask where AI reduces or eliminates them. The AI cart goes after the business model horse.

4A

Governance framework

Automate · Approve · Audit · Assist. Where you stop is determined by risk level and reversibility. Governance is a living system — Approve migrates to Automate as trust builds.

WINS

WINS framework

Generative AI operates with Words, Images, Numbers, Sounds. Assess your business model by what % operates in WINS and how digitized it already is. High-WINS sectors face the most disruption and opportunity.

70%

The iceberg (Korst)

Most organizations focus on the visible 30% — tech, platforms, data. The hidden 70% that actually drives success: strategic clarity, people & skills, org processes, culture & mindset.

The 4A governance framework

Two questions determine which A to choose: (1) If this goes wrong, who gets hurt and how badly? (2) Can we undo it quickly? Stop earlier when risk is high or reversibility is low. Perception of risk changes over time — Approve can migrate to Automate as trust builds.

A

Automate

Fully autonomous with real-time monitoring + guardrails. Low-risk, high-frequency, standardized tasks. T-Mobile: >100M automated network optimization changes/year.

A

Approve

AI acts; human reviews before execution. High-risk but reviewable. T-Mobile: customer offer drafting — AI generates, human approves before sending.

A

Audit

AI acts autonomously; human reviews a sample after. Medium-risk, high-volume tasks where pre-approval isn't feasible at scale.

A

Assist

AI suggests or drafts; human makes the final decision. High-stakes, novel, or irreversible decisions. Most knowledge work starts here.

"Stop earlier when the decision risk is high or reversibility is low. Stop later when errors are cheap and decisions are reversible."

— Session 3, Prof. Netessine

AI process transformation engine

The biggest mistake: automating tasks without redesigning the underlying decision logic. If a human is needed to hand things from A to B, that's a process defect. Three common failure modes: automating tasks not decisions, ignoring the unhappy path, and ignoring end-to-end flow.

1

Pick the decision + define primary KPI

What specific decision is being made? What one metric proves this is working?

2

Redesign workflow — map happy and unhappy paths

Completely rethink the process to get full utility from AI. Don't just speed up the existing steps.

3

Set governance — choose the right 'A'

Assign Assist / Approve / Audit / Automate based on risk and reversibility. Draw the stop line before you build.

4

Measure with 1 KPI + 2–3 guardrails

Avoid Goodhart's Law. "Hours saved only matters when applied to the bottleneck." Measure flow, quality, and economics — not activity.

5

Scale patterns

Recommend → Approve → Execute. Ship a playbook with policy, thresholds, logs, and training. Repeatable patterns beat one-off wins.

Cross-cutting themes

Seven patterns that emerged consistently across all sessions, site visits, and speakers. These were not taught — they were observed.

1

AI is a strategy accelerator, not a strategy itself

Define the business problem first; then ask how AI helps. Starting with AI leads to solutions in search of problems. Jeremy Korst, T-Mobile, Amazon, and Netessine all named this independently. PwC 2026: 56% of CEOs report zero financial return from AI in the last 12 months.

2

The core commoditizes — value lives in the crust

Foundation models will commoditize. Defensibility comes from proprietary data, redesigned workflows, and culture. Madrona: value is currently accruing to the model layer — but this will shift. Companies competing on models alone will lose.

3

Workflow redesign is the real unlock — not task automation

Layering AI onto existing processes captures a fraction of the value. T-Mobile (IVR → AI-native) and Amazon both learned this the hard way. "If a human is needed to hand things from A to B, that's a process defect."

4

Culture eats AI strategy for breakfast

The limiting factor at every company was people's willingness to change — not the technology. Amazon, T-Mobile, Microsoft, and Gates Foundation all named this their biggest challenge. Algorithmic aversion is real. Amazon's golden toilet award: reward experimentation, not outcomes — because outcomes are rare.

5

"How can I get to yes?" — customer obsession as north star

Both T-Mobile and Amazon independently used this exact phrase. Every AI investment filtered through: does this make the customer's life better? "AI is great at disregarding org boundaries — reconsider role definitions."

6

Adaptable people define who wins

AI amplifies skill differences — strong performers get dramatically stronger, rigid performers fall further behind. The most valuable capability going forward is the 'agency quotient.' Everyone is going to have to be a manager of AI agents and humans. Getting there requires classic management skills: delegation, decomposing problems, inspecting results, feedback loops.

7

First movers win because they learn faster

Early movers collect data sooner, learn quicker, and improve faster. Internal data is a compounding moat. The wait-and-see path feels safer but compounds disadvantage. Netessine: doubling experiments per year doubles inventiveness. Amazon typically runs experiments for 5–7 years.

Operations

Apply the 4A framework to each AI use case

For every current or planned AI deployment, explicitly assign Assist / Approve / Audit / Automate based on risk and reversibility. Define trust controls: who signs the policy? When must AI route to a human?

›

Launch one bounded pilot using the 5-step engine

Pick one decision. Redesign the workflow (not just the task). Set governance. Define 1 KPI + 2–3 guardrails. Run controlled before scaling.

›

Replace soft ROI with business outcomes

"Hours saved only matters when applied to the bottleneck." Identify the one business metric your AI deployment directly moves. Measure that. Meetings and success stories are not KPIs.

›
People

Measure team productivity, not individual

Teams are systems: people + tools + AI setup. Same rigor, different author. Metric: shipment deployments per week, not lines of code.

›

Close the VP–middle manager perception gap

Korst data: VPs are 2× more likely to say their org is adopting AI quickly vs. middle managers (56% vs. 28%). Find where the disconnect is — it's often change management, not technical understanding.

›

Onboard AI as a new teammate, not a tool

Amazon's SLT framing: think of AI as a new teammate — org context, team missions, tools to access. Treat AI fluency as a skillset for the whole team, not a specialty for a few.

›

Amazon's golden toilet award: reward the team that runs the most experiments, not the ones that succeed. Create the portfolio: early, mid, and late-stage experiments running simultaneously.
